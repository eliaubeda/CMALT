Core Area 2:
Teaching, learning and assessment processes

a) An understanding of teaching, learning and/or assessment processes

While doing my PhD research I took part in the York Learning and Teaching Award(YLTA), a taught course to reflect on and develop pedagogical practice . Through this course I developed a pedagogical understanding of my teaching practice and became an Associate Fellow of the Higher Education Academy. This was the beginning of an intense learning journey that continues to this day.

In 2020/2021 I designed and delivered a second-year undergraduate module at the Universitat de Barcelona (UB) on the history of theatre. When designing the course, my main concern was to effectively incorporate Active Learning into my lessons in an online environment (see in CA1).

Incorporating Active Learning

Active learning encompasses a wide range of pedagogical practices that encourage learners to work with concepts, engaging in higher order thinking and deep learning to actively build their own knowledge and understanding (Biggs and Tang, 2011; Brame, 2016).

My approach to education is shaped by my ongoing feminist commitment, which I apply to my work, life and pedagogy. In a nutshell, to me feminism is an active fight against all forms of discrimination and exploitation, which is why I endeavour to make my workplace and my work as inclusive as possible. As a feminist educator, I prioritise a student-centred teaching practice to empower students to develop their learning and skills. (E&U) As an extension of this, empathy plays a big role in how I approach my work. When planning for the UB course, I pulled from my own experience as a UB alumna and knew that the students would have next to no experience in Active Learning within the classroom. To this date, there is no pre-class reading, no in-class activities and no in-class active engagement. I experienced a cultural shock when doing my MA in Women’s Studies in the UK: all knowledge acquisition was done through preparatory readings and learning sessions were exclusively guided discussions with our lecturers. Although it was a steep learning curve, I quickly realised that my MA learning was deeper and more meaningful than that of my undergraduate studies.

Nonetheless, I have also seen the pitfalls of Student Lead Learning approach that disregards students’ prior knowledge and level of expertise (Kirschner and Hendrick, 2020a, 2020b). As a GTA in History of Art (UoY), I lead a course where students were expected to describe and analyse artworks without any prior contextual knowledge acquisition.

The students in that module (for all GTAs) struggled to engage meaningfully with the artworks. This was because we were asking students to work with and apply concepts we had not introduced or explained to them. This experience made me realise the importance of providing students with a solid knowledge base (via readings or lectures) to later apply into deeper learning.

Because of experience as both a teacher and a student, I wanted to incorporate the pedagogy I had learnt in the UK (LfC) to the lecture-based teaching my students would be used to (E&U). I wanted to:

  • Combine reception learning with active learning.

    Dividing the sessions in two parts: a short lecture to provide contextual knowledge and a breakout room activity to analyse a theatrical text.

  • Adjust the students’ expectations and make mine very clear.

    I adjusted the students’ expectations for the module: I wouldn’t just provide them information, but I would give them a short pre-reading task for them to analyse collaboratively in-class.

  • Create a safe space where students felt confident to share their views.

    To build up their confidence, I asked students to work in small breakout rooms and share the group’s thoughts in a Padlet (see CA 1). Students could share their thoughts anonymously without the pressure of speaking in front of me (the lecturer) or their peers. We still came together for whole-group discussion but if there were no volunteers from a given group, I would comment on their anonymous contributions. (E&U)

There was a short period of transition as:

  • This was the first time they were asked to contribute to class and share their opinions.
  • They were not used to preparatory reading and some students did not do the reading for the first few sessions. I asked students if they had read the material and encouraged them to be honest, so I could group students effectively.
  • For many of them this was their first time using breakout rooms.

Moreover, I had not foreseen that the university-provided laptops did not have a webcam or microphone and I had to quickly provide instructions on how to join using a phone.

I was not satisfied with the phone-in solution as it could potentially add a financial burden to students, particularly because it only affected those who depended on the university-provided laptops and thus might be at an economic disadvantage. Unfortunately, that was a limitation of Blackboard Collaborate which, unlike Teams or Zoom, did not offer the option to receive a call to join.

After a few sessions of adaptation, the students became more engaged in the analysis and generally came prepared to class.

Designing a course

For this module, I was given three Teaching Objectives. There is a significant difference between TOs and LOs: while the former state what the teacher plans to achieve, the later state what the students should achieve (Kennedy, 2007; Williamson, n. d.). Wanting to deliver a more student-centred teaching, I devised new LOs that would shift the perspective from my teaching to the students’ learning and that could be assessed.

To convert the TOs into LOS, I considered:

  • What can students do with that knowledge?
  • How will the knowledge be assessed?

Example

Old TO

‘Knowing that material and technological aspects had a big impact in theatre’s development’

Followed by

Thinking around the TO

What can students do with that knowledge?

Recognise how changes in theatre history have been influenced by technological advances.

How will the knowledge be assessed?

  • Can they name the key technological advances in the relevant time period? Falling in the ‘Understand’ category of Bloom’s taxonomy (Anderson et al., 2001).

  • Can they explain their impact on the development of theatrical arts? Falling in the ‘Analyse’ category of Bloom’s taxonomy (Anderson et al., 2001).

Followed by

Rewritten LO

‘Identify the main technological advances and explain their impact to Western theatrical arts from the Renaissance onwards’. (E&U)

With the new LOs, I was able to select relevant texts for the students to analyse and hone in the content to cover during the lectures.

In hindsight, although the LOs were effective and helped me design the course, I should have also taken into account the assessment when selecting relevant texts for the students to analyse. The assessed was split between a podcast-essay and an exam – neither of which I marked. The exam questions were only set four weeks in advance at my request, but the podcast-essay questions were handed to each student ¬at the beginning of the term. If I had taken into consideration those essay questions I would have been able to align my content better and prevent some confusion that later arose for students with ambiguous questions. (KUTD)

b) An understanding of your target learners

Accessibility workshops

In September 2022 I restarted the Accessibility workshops series at King’s Online. Accessibility is a key quality value at KO, but we hadn’t had a workshop on it since March 2021. The aim is to keep our accessibility knowledge relevant and up-to-date, as well as sharing our concerns and best practices (C&D, LfC).

Colleagues from different roles attend the workshops with different perspectives, aims and needs.

  • General knowledge.

    Those interacting with faculty and working with the first drafts of the content need a general understanding of the principles of web accessibility to identify issues early on and address them with faculty members.

  • Specific knowledge.

    Those building the content and uploading it on Moodle need specific and technical knowledge on how to meet standards.

I balance these perspectives when designing workshops.

Example

I ran two very different workshops on the same topic: UK legal requirements for accessibility in HE.

For the build team

This informal session was for my immediate team (content developers and visual designers). I went over highlighted sections of the UK Accessibility Regulations with my colleagues, summarising and clarifying them and their significance for our work.

When covering the WCAG 2.1 standards, I used the WCAG website to explain their structure and conformance levels. Because the build team need to know and apply the technical requirements when coding and doing Quality Assurance, I went over each WCAG Guideline and covered in detail a few relevant Success Criteria. (C&D)

For the whole KO team

For this session I summarised the relevant legal points and supported them with quotes from government guidance and King’s accessibility statement. Aiming to keep learners engaged, I included a break-out room exercise where I asked them to consider how a module lead might help a student facing learning barriers due to inaccessible technology. (C&D, KUTD)

I was quite worried the session for the wider team would be too dry and boring. From the feedback I received, I believe I was successful in explaining the legal requirements from KO’s perspective and work processes in a simple and engaging way.

That was a model of how to make a workshop/info session engaging and clear.

ELCD mentorship

In 22/23 I piloted an ELCD mentorship where I mentored a KO learning designer in the E-Learning Content Developer role.

For this pilot we mirrored the already existing Learning Designer mentorship (LfC). The first step was talking with the mentee to establish the outcomes they wanted from the mentorship. Then we arranged some sessions covering the mentee’s interests.

This unstructured approach with no firm outcomes or deadlines did not work well because the ELCD role is very specific:

  • Building content in HTML.
  • Doing BQA on other ELCD’s work.

So there weren’t that many interests from the mentee to address through informal discussion.

After seeking advice, I re-thought the mentorship as a scalable project with a clear and assessable Learning Outcome (LfC): '• By the end of the mentorship, learners will have built a KEATS space following the King’s Online Design System (DS) with examples of the most common components and activities.'

The King’s Online Design System is a set of principles, instructions and toolkit for modular, consistent and reusable design used across all of our programmes/courses.


The aim is to provide mentees with the opportunity to experience the KO workflow and DS from the ELCD perspective. To that effect I:

  • Lead the build team to:

    • Gather relevant guidance and resources,

    • Agree on a structure for the mentorship:

      • Stage 1: Familiarising with the DS.

      • Stage 2: Buddy Quality Assurance (BQA).

      • Stage 3: building content following the DS.

    • Create mock content for mentees to BQA with an accompanying log of issues in it.

  • Designed a mock module for mentees to build content. The module has three units, scaffolding the components from most to least used and by difficulty. (E&U)

Table arranging Moodle activities and components for 'Unit 1', taking into consideration difficulty and noting where they appear in the 'ELCD mentorship' course.

This approach has allowed us to assess the results of the mentorship and scale the project up to include four new mentees to the programme.
